## The Cadence: How the Loop Tightens

The workspace is not a factory line. It is a loop that tightens as the problem clarifies.

This is where the OODA insight becomes most powerful. Boyd did not argue for faster decisions — he argued for faster *reorientation.* The side that can update its mental model of reality faster than the opponent can update theirs will win. Speed is a side effect of alignment, not the goal.

The same principle applies to problem-solving with AI. The first pass through the workspace is slow by design. You are building the structure — collecting raw material, defining frames, establishing context. This is foundational work that cannot be rushed without paying for it later. **90% of wasted compute comes from running Work before Frame and Context are stable.**

But once the structure exists, subsequent passes accelerate dramatically. The frame is established. The context is curated. New iterations draw from verified knowledge instead of raw noise. Reviews are faster because the criteria are explicit. Feedback is precise because the failure gradient tells you exactly where to loop back.

### The Layer Cadence

Each layer has an owner, a trigger, and a rhythm. Respect all three or the system collapses.

**Human → Raw.** The human dumps continuously. No structure required. This layer is always open, always accepting input. New interview, new data point, new screenshot — toss it in. The cadence is: whenever you encounter something relevant.

**Human + AI → Frame.** Collaborative, triggered only when enough raw material accumulates to warrant (re)framing. The frame should not be built early. Wait until patterns emerge from Raw. Wait until you have enough evidence to construct at least two plausible framings. The cadence is: infrequently, deliberately, usually at the start of an investigation and again when evidence invalidates the current frame.

**AI → Context.** Auto-maintained, triggered after the frame stabilizes. Once you know what problem you are solving, the AI can synthesize the relevant knowledge into the five context files. The cadence is: after every significant new input (research finding, decision, completed iteration). The AI proposes updates; the human reviews weekly.

**AI → Work.** Solutions generate on explicit request only. Never auto-run. The prompt should reference the current frame and context explicitly — no ambient context, no "you know what I mean." The cadence is: when the team is ready to probe a hypothesis. Each iteration is a deliberate experiment, not an open-ended generation.

**Human → Review.** Judge, approve, reject. No delegation. The human decides what is good enough, what needs rework, and what needs re-framing. The cadence is: after every iteration. No iteration should exist without a corresponding review.

### The Two Speeds

The workspace operates at two speeds, and understanding the shift between them is crucial.

**Speed 1: Structure building.** The first pass through Raw → Frame → Context is slow, exploratory, and expensive. For our conversion problem, the team spent two weeks collecting raw material, another week defining two frames, and several days building the initial context. This feels inefficient. It is not. It is the investment that makes everything after it fast.

**Speed 2: Tight iteration.** Once the structure exists, the loop tightens to Work → Review → (minor adjustments to Context) → Work. Iterations that took days now take hours. The AI already has the frame, already has the context, already has the history of what was tried. Each new prompt is a precise request operating within established constraints, not a cold-start explanation of the entire problem.

The transition from Speed 1 to Speed 2 is the moment the workspace starts paying dividends. Teams that give up during Speed 1 — "this is too much process" — never experience Speed 2. Teams that skip Speed 1 entirely — jumping straight to AI-generated Work — operate in a permanent Speed 1, re-explaining the problem every session, never building the structural capital that enables acceleration.

### When the Loop Jumps

Not all feedback is equal. Sometimes Review sends you back to the previous iteration. Sometimes it sends you back to the beginning.

A small failure — the proposed UX change has a missing edge case — loops back to Work. This is a tight, fast correction.

A medium failure — the A/B test results show the UX change had no effect on conversion — loops back to Context (update the facts) and potentially Frame (reconsider whether this is actually a UX problem).

A large failure — a customer interview reveals the product has a billing bug that prevents payment entirely — loops all the way back to Raw. New observations. New framing. The entire investigation may pivot.

The workspace supports all three. It does not prescribe a fixed path. It provides the structure for any path to be legible, traceable, and reversible.

### The OODA Mapping

The parallel to Boyd's OODA loop is not metaphorical — it is structural:

- **Observe** = Raw (gather unprocessed inputs from the environment)
- **Orient** = Frame + Context (synthesize observations into an actionable understanding)
- **Decide** = Review (evaluate options and commit to action)
- **Act** = Work (execute the chosen approach)

But unlike the classic OODA diagram — often drawn as a simple circle — the workspace makes the *feedback paths* explicit. Orient does not just feed into Decide. It is continuously updated by the results of Action. Decide does not just trigger Action — a decision to reject can send you back to Observe (new data needed), to Orient (reframing required), or to a different Action entirely.

This is Boyd's actual model, not the simplified version. And it is exactly what the workspace implements: a multi-path feedback system where the gradient of the error determines the size of the loop.

> The cadence is non-linear. Early iterations leap across layers. Later iterations pulse in tight feedback loops between Work and Review.

Speed is a side effect of alignment. Not the goal.

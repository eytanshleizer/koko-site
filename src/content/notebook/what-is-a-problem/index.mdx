We use the word *problem* as if we all mean the same thing. We do not. And the difference between what you think a problem is and what it actually is determines whether you solve it, navigate it, or make it worse.

## The common mistake

Most people treat a problem as a puzzle: something with a correct answer, waiting to be found. You identify the pieces, arrange them properly, and the problem dissolves. This works for crosswords. It works for arithmetic. It does not work for the problems that matter.

The problems that matter — the ones worth a life of work — do not have correct answers. They have *better and worse continuations*. They are not solved; they are *moved*.

## A better framing

A problem is a tension between a present state and a possible state, where the path from one to the other is non-obvious and the stakes are real.

That definition has three parts, and each one matters:

1. **Tension, not absence.** A problem is not a missing piece. It is a *pull* — a sense that things could be otherwise, that the current state is unstable or incomplete.
2. **Non-obvious path.** If the solution is clear, you do not have a problem. You have a task. The problem lives in the gap between knowing where you want to go and not knowing how to get there.
3. **Real stakes.** A problem without stakes is a curiosity. The stakes are what make the problem *yours* — what bind your attention to it.

## What a problem is not

A problem is not a question, though it may contain questions. A question has an answer. A problem has a *resolution* — a state in which the tension is reduced, transformed, or redirected.

A problem is not a defect, though it may look like one. Calling a problem a defect assumes there is a correct state to restore. Most interesting problems are not deviations from correctness. They are *emergences* — patterns that arise from complexity and demand a response.

A problem is not an enemy. The reflex to "defeat" a problem leads to short-term fixes that ignore structure. The better stance is to *understand* the problem deeply enough that your response reshapes the landscape that produced it.

## The shape of a real problem

Real problems have texture. They have:

- **Depth.** The surface presentation is rarely the real problem. The real problem is usually one or two layers down, hidden by symptoms and compensations.
- **History.** Problems do not arrive fully formed. They accumulate. They are the result of previous decisions, previous solutions, previous avoidances.
- **Context.** A problem is never just the thing. It is the thing *in a situation*, surrounded by constraints, stakeholders, and competing values.
- **Dynamics.** Problems change as you work on them. The act of intervening alters the problem. This is why linear plans fail: the problem you planned for is not the problem you encounter.

## What this means for problem-solving

If a problem is a tension with a non-obvious path and real stakes, then problem-solving is not about finding answers. It is about:

- **Framing well.** The quality of the resolution depends almost entirely on the quality of the frame. A problem framed poorly will resist every solution.
- **Holding ambiguity.** The discomfort of not knowing is part of the work. Rushing to clarity prematurely collapses the problem into a caricature of itself.
- **Acting to learn.** You cannot think your way through a real problem. You have to intervene, observe, and adjust. The loop is: act, see, reframe, act again.
- **Knowing when to stop.** Not every problem needs to be solved completely. Sometimes the right resolution is to make the problem legible enough that someone else can continue, or to transform it into a different problem entirely.

## Problems and AI

This framing changes how we think about AI and problem-solving.

If a problem is a puzzle with a correct answer, then AI is a near-perfect solver. It can search the space of answers faster than any human.

But if a problem is a tension that requires framing, holding ambiguity, acting to learn, and knowing when to stop — then AI is a partner, not a replacement. It can generate possibilities, surface patterns, and accelerate the loop. But it cannot feel the stakes. It cannot hold the ambiguity with the same weight. It cannot know when the resolution is *enough*.

The question is not whether AI can solve problems. The question is what kind of problem-solving we want to preserve as human work — and what we are willing to hand over.

## The short version

A problem is not a question. It is a tension between what is and what could be, where the path is unclear and the stakes are real. You do not solve it. You move it. You reshape it. You learn to live with it differently.

The best problem-solvers are not the ones with the best answers. They are the ones who hold the problem most carefully — who refuse to simplify it prematurely, who keep their hands on it long enough to feel its real shape, and who know that the resolution is not the end but a *better continuation*.

The interesting product category that hasn't been built yet is the one that helps you decide *which* problem to work on. Not project management. Not analytics. Something more like a notebook for the upstream half of work — where problems get caught, framed, ranked, and handed off.

Call it problem intelligence. This is a sketch of what it might feel like to use one.

## The inbox

Problems arrive from many places — support volume, dashboards, conversations, half-formed observations. Most of them aren't yet problems; they're complaints, signals, hunches. The first job is to catch them and let them sit somewhere visible while they take shape.

A few things are deliberate in this view:

- **State as a first-class field.** A problem has a *framing* state, then *surveying*, then *framed*, then *solving*. The state isn't a label you set; it's where the problem actually is in the work. Most ticketing systems treat status as either "open" or "done." That's missing the whole upstream half.
- **Detail is metadata, not free text.** The line under each title is structured — number of hypotheses, related problems, signals correlated. The system can compute most of it; the human curates.
- **Frames are reusable.** Once you've framed "user retention is dropping" four times, the framing template is itself an object you can reach for.

<Callout title="The shift" variant="accent">
This isn't an AI product. It's an interface for the human side of an AI-native workflow — the part where you decide what to point the agents at, and where you keep track of what came back.
</Callout>

## The map

The other view that matters is structural — how problems and concepts connect. Same data, different camera. Drag the nodes around if you want.

A force-directed graph isn't a deep insight, but it is the right *shape* for this content. Problems aren't a list. They're a network with weak connections, dependencies, shared context. Some clusters are solvable units; some are vague terrain you keep coming back to. Seeing the network changes which problems you pick up next.

A few things this view does well that a list doesn't:

- **It surfaces clusters.** When five problems all link to the same node, that node is probably worth its own frame.
- **It exposes orphans.** Problems with no connections are usually either trivial or deeply important — both are worth a look.
- **It handles partial structure.** You don't have to know how everything connects to start. Add an edge as you notice it.

## What's actually different

Most software for "thinking work" is either an editor or a dashboard. Problem intelligence is neither. It's an interface for the *between* state — when something is more than a thought and less than a project, when the right move is to keep noticing it rather than to act.

The pieces aren't novel. Inbox views, graph views, structured notes have all been done. What's new is the assumption that **the upstream half of work is now the work** — and that it deserves its own first-class tool, not a shared corner of Notion.

## Why a system, not a model

A model takes a prompt and returns a completion. That's a transaction. Real problem solving is not a transaction. It's a loop.

The problems worth solving are:

- **Ill-structured at the start.** The right question is itself part of the work. The first answer to "what's the problem?" is almost always wrong.
- **Iterative.** What you find at step two changes what you ask at step three. There is no path you can pre-plan.
- **Embedded.** They live in context the model doesn't have memorized — your data, your constraints, your prior work, the half-decisions made by people who left.
- **Underdetermined.** There isn't one right answer; there's a set of answers ranked by criteria you may not have stated yet.

Try to collapse all of that into a single prompt. You either pre-bake the entire solution into the prompt — which means you've already done the work and the model is just typing it for you — or you get a generic answer that doesn't fit your situation.

> **The shift.** The AI-native question isn't *which model is smart enough?* It's *which system is structured enough to let the model contribute on the parts where it can?*

To actually solve a non-trivial problem with AI, you need:

1. A small cast of agents with distinct roles.
2. Memory that persists between turns.
3. Tools that observe the world.
4. A loop that knows when to stop.

Those four pieces are what the rest of this post is about.

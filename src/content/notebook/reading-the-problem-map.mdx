When you hand a problem to an agent, you're not just transferring a task. You're transferring a worldview — the boundary, the vocabulary, the success criterion. Most of the bad outputs I see come from a mis-shape of that handoff, not from the model.

## What gets lost in the handoff

Three things, almost always.

<Callout title="Note">
The agent only sees what's in the prompt. Anything that lives in your head — the constraints you take for granted, the audience you're speaking to, the trade-off you've already made — has to be made explicit.
</Callout>

The first is **the boundary**. You know what's in scope and what's not. The agent doesn't.

The second is **the vocabulary**. Every domain has terms with precise meaning. "User" doesn't mean the same thing in payments as it does in marketing copy.

The third is **the test**. You usually have a fuzzy sense of "I'll know it when I see it." That isn't enough.

## A small frame

I find it useful to write out four things before starting any non-trivial agent task:

```
Goal:        what would "done" look like?
Inputs:      what does the agent have access to?
Constraints: what's off-limits, and why?
Test:        how will we know we got it right?
```

That's not a process; it's a lens. Even thirty seconds of writing those four lines changes the quality of the output enough to be worth the time.

<Stat value="4×" label="quality bump from a 30-second framing pass" />

## When the frame is wrong

Sometimes the frame is wrong before the work starts, and no agent in the world can recover from that.

<Aside label="Common case">
You're asking the agent to optimize something, but the thing you actually want is not what's being optimized. Reframe what "good" means before generating anything.
</Aside>

The fix isn't to keep iterating the prompt. It's to step back to the frame and check whether the problem you've handed over is the problem you actually have.

> The frame is the unit of the new work.

## The work, more carefully

The promise of working with AI isn't that it solves more problems. It's that we get to spend the saved time *thinking better about which problems to solve, and how*. The map matters more than the move.

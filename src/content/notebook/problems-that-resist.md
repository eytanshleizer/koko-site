There is a class of problem that rewards exploration but punishes solving. You can map it. You can trace its edges. You can spend a decade with it and learn its weather. But you cannot finish it, because the problem was never the kind of thing that ends.

## Why the framing matters

If you treat an open-ended problem as a closed one, you optimize for the wrong outcome. You ship an answer, declare victory, and watch the situation reorganize around your answer in a way that makes the original problem worse. The classic move is to mistake a *condition* for a *defect*.

> The interesting questions resist answers — and the cost of being wrong is paid upstream of the work.

That cost is the part most teams underprice.

## What changes when you accept it

Three things, mostly:

- **You build instruments instead of solutions.** An instrument is something that lets you keep working on the problem after the project ends.
- **You measure progress as resolution, not closure.** You're not looking for the answer; you're looking for a clearer picture.
- **You write more.** Open problems live on language. The clearer your writing, the further you can carry the problem.

I'll write more about the practical side of this — the actual instruments — soon. For now, the framing is the thing.

## The Five-Layer Model

Information has gravity. It also has velocity.

**Gravity** is the tendency of information to settle into a stable, trusted state. Raw interview notes have low gravity — they are unstable, unverified, and subject to reinterpretation. A verified fact in the Context layer has high gravity — it has been checked, sourced, and is relied upon by everything downstream. Gravity increases as information moves through the layers.

**Velocity** is how frequently information changes. Raw inputs arrive continuously — new interviews, new data exports, new observations. The Frame changes less frequently — only when you fundamentally redefine the problem. Context evolves steadily as understanding deepens. Work iterates in bursts. Review decisions are infrequent and consequential.

Mix high-velocity noise with high-gravity truth and you get mediocre decisions. The five layers exist to prevent this contamination.

> **Raw → Frame → Context → Work → Review**

Each layer has three properties: a **purpose** (what kind of information it holds), a **cadence** (how fast it changes), and an **editor** (who writes, who reads, and who decides). These three properties together constitute the layer's *governance* — and governance is what separates a workspace from a folder.

Here is the model in brief:

**Raw** is unprocessed input. Human-only. The AI does not touch it. This is your messy thinking space — interviews, screenshots, notes, articles, half-formed ideas. It is supposed to be disorganized. That is the point.

**Frame** is the structure you impose on that input. Human-led, AI-assisted. This is where you define what problem you are actually solving, what constraints you are operating under, who the stakeholders are, and what questions remain open. The AI's role here is to critique, clarify, and stress-test — not to write the frame for you.

**Context** is the curated knowledge the agent needs to reason well. Joint authorship — the AI maintains it, the human reviews it. This is the minimum viable orientation: a glossary, verified facts, a decisions log, and pattern recognition. This is what the agent reads before doing any work.

**Work** is the artifact being produced. AI-led, human-edited. This is where solutions are generated, prototypes are built, analyses are written. Each attempt is a numbered iteration, not a draft. The naming matters.

**Review** is the feedback loop that closes the cycle. Human judges, AI evaluates. This is where you decide if the work is good, if the frame was right, if the context was complete. Review is the layer that determines whether the next loop is a tight refinement or a fundamental re-frame.

### Why Five Layers, Not Three or Seven

The number is not arbitrary. Each layer represents a distinct *epistemic state* — a fundamentally different relationship between you, the information, and the AI.

Three layers would collapse Frame and Context into one, losing the crucial distinction between "how we defined the problem" and "what we know about the domain." These are different things, maintained by different people, at different cadences. Merging them creates governance confusion: who is responsible for the problem definition? Who is responsible for the facts?

Seven or more layers would introduce distinctions without governance differences. If two layers have the same editor, the same cadence, and the same kind of content, they should be one layer. The test for a layer's existence is: **does it have a different answer to "who writes, who reads, who decides"?** If yes, it is a distinct layer. If no, it is a subfolder.

### The Governance Principle

Every layer answers three questions:

1. **Who writes?** — Who is authorized to create and modify content in this layer?
2. **Who reads?** — Who consumes this information and for what purpose?
3. **Who decides?** — Who has authority to approve, reject, or promote information from this layer?

These are not suggestions. They are constraints. When an AI agent modifies raw interview transcripts, the system has failed — not because the edit was wrong, but because the governance boundary was violated. When a human writes the Context layer from scratch instead of reviewing the AI's synthesis, efficiency collapses. When no one reviews the Work layer before iterating, quality drifts.

The boundaries between layers are load-bearing walls, not decorative lines. Remove one and the structure above it collapses.

> **Key insight:** The layers are not folders. They are contracts about who writes, who reads, and who decides.
